## 1. Writing Style & Voice

### Narrative / Thematic Memos (Investor Letter Style)

Write like a **thoughtful, experienced investor writing to intelligent colleagues**. The tone is:

- **First-person**: "I believe...", "I've been thinking about...", "I recently learned..."
- **Conversational but precise**: Plain language, no jargon without explanation
- **Intellectually honest**: Acknowledge what you do NOT know
- **Personal**: Share anecdotes, observations, and reflections
- **Progressive**: Build an argument step by step; each paragraph flows naturally into the next
- **Humble**: "I could be wrong...", "This is one way to think about it..."

Key techniques:
- Start with why you're writing NOW: "When I was preparing to write my December memo...", "I recently returned to this topic because..."
- Use bold for sentences that carry the argument forward: "**First, there's the pace at which developments are occurring.**"
- Ask provocative questions the reader may not have considered
- Draw on historical parallels and analogies
- Use indented italic blocks for third-party quotes or extended excerpts
- End sections with a clear takeaway

### Structured / Deal Memos (Top-Tier VC Style)

Write like an **analyst presenting a clear investment case**:

- **Direct and concise**: Every sentence advances the investment case or flags a risk
- **Bullet-oriented**: Use lists for parallel items (problems, competitors, risks)
- **Fact-forward**: Lead with the most important fact, then explain
- **Metric-aware**: Include specific numbers (valuation, ownership, growth rates)
- **Recommendation-first**: State the recommendation clearly, then support it

Key techniques:
- Start with a one-sentence thesis in the opening paragraph
- Use bold section headings for each analysis area
- Present data as inline text or bullet lists, not tables
- Include specific deal terms (amount, valuation, ownership)
- Highlight key risks as bullet points with inline severity
- End with a clear recommendation and next steps

## 2. Investment Thesis Development

### The Core Question

Every investment memo answers one of these questions:
- **Venture/PE**: "Why will this company generate outsized returns?"
- **Public equity**: "Why is the market mispricing this security?"
- **Thematic**: "What is happening in [sector/theme] and what should investors do?"

### Thesis Structure (Venture/PE)

A well-formed thesis has three components:

1. **What**: What does the company do? (1 sentence)
2. **Why it wins**: What is the durable competitive advantage? (1-2 sentences)
3. **Return profile**: At what valuation, what ownership, what exit path? (1 sentence)

Example (early-stage video platform): "[Company] is a user-generated video platform that leverages streaming technology and community features to dominate a nascent market. Its first-mover advantage in UGC video, combined with network effects and low-cost viral distribution, positions it to become the primary video destination on the Internet. We propose a $1M seed investment at a pre-money valuation that yields ~30% ownership post-Series A."

### Thesis Structure (Public Equity)

1. **Mispricing**: What does the market get wrong? (2-3 sentences)
2. **Catalyst**: What will cause the market to re-rate? (1-2 sentences)
3. **Downside protection**: What limits losses if wrong? (1 sentence)

### Thesis Structure (Thematic)

1. **Observation**: What is changing and why? (2-3 sentences)
2. **Implication**: What does this mean for asset prices/portfolios? (2-3 sentences)
3. **Uncertainty**: What could prove this thesis wrong? (1-2 sentences)

## 3. Venture/PE Deal Analysis

### Market Sizing (TAM/SAM/SOM)

Present as plain text, not tables:

TAM is the total spend on the problem. SAM is the addressable slice the company can serve. SOM is what the company can realistically capture in 5 years (typically 5-20%). TAM should be bottom-up validated, not just top-down.

### Competition Analysis

For each competitor, assess: direct vs. indirect, stage/scale, strengths, weaknesses, threat level. Present as bullet lists with brief inline assessments.

**Competitive moat types** to identify: network effects, switching costs, economies of scale, brand/trust, data moat, regulatory/IP barriers.

### Unit Economics

| Metric | Target | Red Flag |
|--------|--------|----------|
| LTV / CAC ratio | > 3x | < 2x |
| CAC payback period | < 18 months | > 36 months |
| Gross margin | > 60% (software) | < 40% |
| Net revenue retention | > 100% | < 90% |

Present in bullet form or inline text, not as a formatted table.

### Team Assessment

Evaluate: domain expertise, prior success, execution ability, coachability, motivation, gaps.

### Risk Assessment Framework

Score each risk on probability and impact. Categories: market, execution, technology, competitive, financing, regulatory, macro, team.

## 4. Public Equity Analysis

### Financial Analysis Checklist

Include: revenue growth (3-5 years), gross margin trend, operating margin, EBITDA, cash flow, balance sheet strength. Present as inline text with bold key figures, not as formatted tables.

### Valuation Methodology

Use 2-3 methods: trading comps, DCF, sum-of-the-parts. Triangulate.

### Key Questions

1. What is the market expecting? (price-implied growth)
2. Bull case vs. bear case?
3. Margin of safety?
4. Catalysts in 6-12 months?
5. What could cause permanent capital loss?

## 5. Thematic/Macro Analysis

### Framework

1. Define the phenomenon precisely
2. Historical context — has this happened before?
3. Current state with evidence
4. Drivers (technology, policy, demographics, behavior)
5. Future trajectory (base/bull/bear scenarios)
6. Investment implications
7. Risks to the thesis

### Narrative Memo Stylistic Elements

- Intellectually honest: acknowledge uncertainty
- Historical perspective: draw parallels to past cycles
- Balanced argument: present bull AND bear cases
- Plain language: explain complex concepts accessibly
- Provocative questions
- Bottom-line clarity even when the answer is "it depends"

## 6. Common Analytical Frameworks

### Porter's Five Forces

Assess: competitive rivalry, supplier power, buyer power, threat of substitution, threat of new entry.

### SWOT Analysis

Present as a simple indented block, not a formatted table, using bullet points under Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats headings.

### Scenario Planning

Define 3 scenarios: bear (25th percentile), base (50th), bull (75th). Assign probabilities. Present in prose or bullet form.

## 7. Quality Standards

### What Makes a Good Investment Memo

| Dimension | Good | Poor |
|-----------|------|------|
| **Clarity** | Reads well to someone unfamiliar | Jargon-heavy |
| **Balance** | Presents bull and bear cases fairly | One-sided advocacy |
| **Specificity** | Concrete numbers, named competitors | Vague generalizations |
| **Rigor** | Sources cited, logic explicit | Unsupported assertions |
| **Actionability** | Clear recommendation with conditions | Analysis without conclusion |
| **Brevity** | Every paragraph advances the argument | Repetitive or tangential |
| **Voice** | Personal and authentic (narrative) or direct (deal) | Generic, report-like |

### Red Flags to Highlight

Always call out these warning signs explicitly:
- Founder has not worked in the target industry before
- No confirmed customer revenue (pre-revenue at Series B+)
- LTV/CAC calculation excludes key costs
- Market size is "top-down" only
- Fewer than 3 competitors named
- Valuation has no comparable precedent
- Key person risk
- Regulatory approval not yet obtained

### Writing Quality

- Use active voice: "We believe..." not "It is believed that..."
- Short sentences and paragraphs beat long ones
- One idea per paragraph
- Bullet lists for parallel items; prose for narrative flow
- "So what?" test: every fact must connect to the investment thesis
