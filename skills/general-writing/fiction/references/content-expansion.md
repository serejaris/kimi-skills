# Content Expansion

Techniques for expanding a chapter that falls short of its word count target. Used by the orchestrator when `check_wordcount.py` returns FAIL.

**Rule**: Use max 3 techniques per chapter. Every expansion must serve the story — never pad.

---

## When to Expand

Expand when the chapter is short AND the narrative benefits from more depth. Not every short chapter is a problem — some chapters (action climaxes, interlude transitions) are naturally shorter.

**Expand**: chapter is below target and feels rushed, thin, or under-dramatized.
**Do NOT expand**: chapter is below target but tight, propulsive, and complete. In this case, adjust the target or accept the shorter length.

---

## Techniques

### 1. Scene Detail Enrichment

Add sensory and environmental detail to existing scenes. Does not change plot — deepens immersion.

**Method**: Walk through the scene and add what the POV character perceives. Prioritize non-visual senses (sound, smell, touch, taste).

**Before**: He entered the warehouse.
**After**: The warehouse smelled of diesel and old cardboard. Fluorescent tubes buzzed overhead, one of them flickering in a rhythm that felt deliberate. His shoes stuck to the floor with each step.

**When to use**: Scenes that read like stage directions — characters moving through blank space.
**When NOT to use**: Action sequences where speed matters more than atmosphere.

### 2. Internal Monologue

Expand the POV character's thought process during key moments. Reveals psychology, stakes, and conflict.

**Method**: At decision points, pause the action and let the character think. Show the weighing of options, the pull of contradictory desires, the fear of consequences.

**Before**: She decided to lie.
**After**: The truth was right there — she could feel it pressing against her teeth. But the truth would end everything. And she wasn't ready for that. Not yet. Not while she still had something to lose.

**When to use**: Turning points, moral dilemmas, moments of realization.
**When NOT to use**: Mid-action sequences (breaks momentum), trivial decisions.

### 3. Dialogue Expansion

Deepen existing conversations. Add subtext, negotiation, evasion, misunderstanding.

**Method**: Identify dialogue that's purely transactional (info-exchange) and add layers:
- Character A wants something from the conversation
- Character B wants something different
- Neither says what they actually want directly
- The conversation becomes a negotiation beneath the surface

**Before**:
"Where were you last night?" / "At the office."

**After**:
"Where were you last night?" She kept her voice light, as if she were asking about the weather.
"At the office." He didn't look up from his phone. "The Henderson deal."
"Right." She poured herself a glass of water. "Because I called the office at nine. No one picked up."
The phone went dark in his hand.

**When to use**: Scenes where characters have conflicting agendas. Relationship scenes.
**When NOT to use**: Rapid-fire exchanges in action/thriller pacing.

### 4. Sensory Layers

Add a second sensory pass to scenes that only use vision. Layer in sound, texture, temperature, smell, taste.

**Method**: For each scene, identify the dominant sense used (usually sight). Add 2-3 details from other senses.

**Sense palette by scene type**:
| Scene Type | Primary | Add |
|-----------|---------|-----|
| Combat | visual (movement) | pain/impact (touch), breathing/metal (sound), blood (taste/smell) |
| Intimate | visual (faces) | warmth/skin (touch), breath/voice (sound), scent (smell) |
| Investigation | visual (clues) | dust/chemical (smell), paper/fabric (touch), silence (sound) |
| Nature/travel | visual (landscape) | wind/birdsong (sound), temperature (touch), earth/plant (smell) |
| Dining/social | visual (people) | food (taste/smell), ambient noise (sound), tablecloth/glass (touch) |

**When to use**: Any scene that feels "flat" or reads like a screenplay.
**When NOT to use**: Internal monologue sections (sensory overload conflicts with introspection).

### 5. Subplot Weaving

Introduce or advance a subplot within the main chapter's scenes. The subplot intersects with the main plot without derailing it.

**Method**: Identify a subplot thread from `threads.md` that's due for advancement. Weave it into an existing scene:
- A secondary character's line of dialogue hints at their own storyline
- The POV character notices something related to the subplot while focused on the main plot
- A brief subplot scene (500-800 words) inserted between main plot scenes

**When to use**: Mid-volume chapters where the main plot needs breathing room.
**When NOT to use**: Climax chapters (all focus on main thread), opening chapters (too many threads too early).

### 6. Pacing Modulation

Slow down a key moment that's currently compressed. Stretch real-time perception for dramatic effect.

**Method**: Identify the chapter's most important moment (a reveal, a confrontation, a decision). Expand it from 1 paragraph to 3-5 paragraphs by:
- Breaking the moment into micro-beats
- Adding the character's physical sensations
- Slowing time perception ("The next three seconds lasted forever")
- Showing other characters' reactions in sequence

**Before**: The gun fired. He fell.
**After**: The sound wasn't what he expected — not a bang but a flat crack, like a branch snapping. For a moment nothing happened. Then his leg buckled. He looked down and saw the fabric of his trousers darkening, the stain spreading faster than seemed possible. The floor came up to meet him, cold and gritty against his palms.

**When to use**: Climactic moments, reveals, deaths, first kisses, betrayals — any moment the reader should feel in slow motion.
**When NOT to use**: Routine transitions, travel scenes, setup exposition.

### 7. Atmosphere Building

Use environment as emotional mirror. The setting reflects or contrasts the character's internal state.

**Method**: Match (or deliberately mismatch) environmental details to the emotional register of the scene:
- **Pathetic fallacy**: storm during conflict, sunshine during false peace
- **Ironic contrast**: a child's birthday party on the day of a funeral
- **Environmental pressure**: heat, noise, crowds, confined spaces amplifying tension
- **Silence and emptiness**: absence as emotional weight

**When to use**: Transitions between scenes, chapter openings, aftermath scenes.
**When NOT to use**: When the chapter is already atmosphere-heavy (diminishing returns).

---

## Genre-Specific Expansion Priorities

| Genre | Best Techniques | Avoid |
|-------|----------------|-------|
| Literary fiction | Internal monologue, atmosphere, sensory | Subplot weaving (can feel diluting) |
| Thriller / suspense | Pacing modulation, dialogue expansion | Extended internal monologue (kills pace) |
| Romance | Dialogue expansion, sensory layers, internal monologue | Action-scene detail enrichment |
| Fantasy / xianxia | Scene detail, atmosphere, subplot | Over-expanding combat (readers skim long fights) |
| Mystery / detective | Dialogue expansion, scene detail (clue-hiding) | Internal monologue that reveals too much |
| Horror | Sensory layers, atmosphere, pacing modulation | Subplot weaving (breaks dread) |

---

## Expansion Constraints

- **Max 3 techniques per chapter** — more than 3 risks bloating
- **Expansion must serve story** — if removing the expanded content doesn't weaken the chapter, it's padding
- **Maintain tension** — expanded sections must still contain conflict or dramatic question
- **Respect pacing** — don't slow a fast chapter to hit a number; adjust the target instead
- **No exposition padding** — backstory dumps, world-building lectures, and summary narration are not expansion
