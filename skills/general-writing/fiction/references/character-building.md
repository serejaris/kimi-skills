# Character Building

Craft reference for designing fiction characters. Used by `character_designer` (Stage 1) and `fiction_writer` (Stage 2). All character data lives in `characters.md` — this document teaches HOW to build characters, not where to store them.

---

## Core Architecture

Every significant character needs four load-bearing elements. Without any one of them, the character collapses into a plot device.

| Element | Question | Why It Matters |
|---------|----------|---------------|
| **Desire** | What do they want most? | Drives every action and decision |
| **Fear** | What do they avoid or deny? | Creates vulnerability and dramatic irony |
| **Contradiction** | Where are they internally inconsistent? | Makes them feel human, not algorithmic |
| **Arc** | How do they change (or refuse to change)? | Gives the reader a reason to follow them |

**Desire vs Fear** creates the engine of character drama: what they want pulls them forward, what they fear holds them back. The story forces them to choose.

**Contradiction** is what separates characters from archetypes. "Brave in battle, cowardly in love" is more interesting than "brave" alone. Contradictions create scenes where the character surprises the reader without betraying consistency.

## Character Depth Layers

Build characters from inside out:

### Layer 1: Psychology (invisible to other characters)

- **Core wound**: the formative experience that shaped their worldview
- **Belief system**: what they hold as true about the world (often distorted by the wound)
- **Coping mechanism**: how they protect themselves from re-experiencing the wound
- **Blind spot**: what they cannot see about themselves (but the reader can)

### Layer 2: Behavior (visible to other characters)

- **Decision patterns**: what they choose when stakes are high
- **Stress response**: fight / flight / freeze / fawn — and how it manifests physically
- **Social mask**: how they present to the world vs who they actually are
- **Habitual actions**: nervous tics, rituals, comfort behaviors

### Layer 3: Voice (audible in dialogue)

- **Vocabulary**: formal/informal, technical/plain, profane/polite
- **Rhythm**: short declarative vs long winding, interrupts others vs waits patiently
- **Evasion patterns**: what topics they deflect, how they change subjects
- **Verbal signature**: a phrase, a construction, a tone that's uniquely theirs

### Layer 4: External (visible to the reader)

- **Appearance**: 2-3 specific physical details (not generic beauty/handsomeness)
- **Environment**: what their living space looks like (reveals character without telling)
- **Possessions**: what they carry, what they refuse to throw away
- **First impression**: what a stranger would notice in 3 seconds

## Character Types and Functions

### Protagonist Design

The protagonist must earn the reader's investment — not through likability, but through **comprehensibility**. The reader must understand WHY this person acts as they do, even when disagreeing.

**Protagonist requirements**:
- Clear desire that drives the plot forward
- A flaw that creates real (not manufactured) obstacles
- Agency — the protagonist makes choices that matter; things don't just happen to them
- Growth potential — room to change, whether or not they ultimately do

**Protagonist patterns**:

| Pattern | Engine | Risk |
|---------|--------|------|
| Classic hero | External threat + internal growth | Predictability, wish fulfillment |
| Anti-hero | Moral complexity + pragmatism | Reader alienation if pushed too far |
| Reluctant hero | Resistance to the call + circumstantial pressure | Passive protagonist if mishandled |
| Tragic hero | Fatal flaw + escalating consequences | Melodrama if the flaw is too obvious |
| Everyman | Reader proxy + extraordinary circumstances | Blandness if not given specific traits |
| Unreliable narrator | Gap between narration and truth | Confusion if unreliability isn't carefully controlled |

### Antagonist Design

The antagonist is NOT "the evil one." The antagonist is whoever most effectively opposes the protagonist's desire. The best antagonists believe they are the hero of their own story.

**Antagonist requirements**:
- A coherent worldview that justifies their actions (to them)
- Competence — they must be a credible threat
- A connection to the protagonist that makes the conflict personal, not just situational
- Their own desire and fear, independent of the protagonist

**Antagonist motivation spectrum**:
- **Ideological**: genuinely believes in a cause (the most compelling type)
- **Wounded**: past trauma drives destructive behavior (sympathetic but dangerous)
- **Systemic**: represents a system or institution rather than personal malice
- **Mirror**: shares the protagonist's goal but pursues it through opposing methods
- **Nihilistic**: has given up on meaning (hardest to write well — requires philosophical depth)

### Supporting Cast

Every supporting character must pass the **removal test**: if you delete this character, does the story break? If not, merge them with another character or cut them.

**Supporting character functions**:

| Function | What They Do | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Confidant | Gives the protagonist someone to voice thoughts to | Watson, Horatio |
| Foil | Highlights the protagonist's traits through contrast | Draco to Harry |
| Catalyst | Forces change without changing themselves | Manic pixie (done well), mentor |
| Mirror | Shows the protagonist what they could become | Dark mirror villain, cautionary figure |
| Stakes anchor | Someone the protagonist (and reader) cares about losing | Family member, love interest, child |
| Comic relief | Releases tension without deflating stakes | Works best when also serving another function |

## Relationship Design

Characters don't exist in isolation. Every relationship needs:

1. **Asymmetry**: each character wants something different from the relationship
2. **Tension source**: what creates friction (values, history, competing goals, power imbalance)
3. **Evolution**: the relationship must change across the story — no static pairs

**Relationship dynamics worth exploring**:

| Dynamic | Dramatic Fuel | Pitfall |
|---------|--------------|---------|
| Mutual respect + competing goals | Difficult choices, loyalty tests | Can stagnate if conflict is only external |
| One-sided knowledge (secrets) | Dramatic irony, betrayal potential | Feels manipulative if dragged too long |
| Power imbalance | Resentment, obligation, rebellion | Uncomfortable if not handled with awareness |
| Forced alliance | Gradual trust-building, vulnerability | Formulaic if the arc is too predictable |
| Former intimacy (exes, estranged family) | Shared history as weapon and bridge | Melodramatic if every scene is about the past |

## Character Introduction

The first time a character appears on the page defines the reader's relationship with them. Introductions must do double duty: establish who this person IS and hook the reader's curiosity.

**Effective introduction methods**:
- **In action**: character doing something that reveals competence or personality
- **In conflict**: character in opposition with someone or something
- **In contradiction**: character doing something that surprises, then is later explained
- **Through another's eyes**: a POV character's reaction to meeting them (filtered, partial, interesting)

**Banned introduction methods**:
- Mirror self-description
- Resume-style listing (name, age, job, appearance paragraph)
- Waking up / daily routine
- Uncontextualized physical description catalog

**Introduction example contrast**:

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| Li Ming, 28, was a detective. He walked into his office. | Li Ming ducked under the police tape. The uniform tried to stop him. "City CID." He flashed his badge without slowing down and walked straight to the body. |
| 她叫小红，是个很漂亮的女孩，坐在窗边。 | 她已经三天没睡了，咖啡杯里的液体在颤抖。门铃响起时，她差点把杯子摔在地上。 |

## Character Consistency Across Chapters

See [consistency.md](./consistency.md) § Character Consistency for the full protocol. Key points:

- `characters.md` → Current State is the single source of truth
- Behavior must match Desire + Fear + Contradiction
- Voice must persist (vocabulary, rhythm, evasion patterns)
- Deviation requires in-story justification within the same or next chapter
- The Arc field tracks trajectory: `[start state] → [current state] → [projected end]`

## Ensemble Cast Management

For novels with 5+ significant characters:

- **Screen time budget**: not every character appears in every chapter. Plan appearances via the chapter specs in `outline_raw.md`.
- **Rotating focus**: even in single-POV stories, supporting characters cycle in and out. No character should appear in more than 3 consecutive chapters without a break (unless they're the POV character).
- **Distinctive entry**: each character brings a different energy to a scene. If two characters serve the same function, merge them.
- **Relationship web**: every pair of significant characters should have a defined relationship with a tension source (tracked in `characters.md` → Relationships table).
- **Death and departure**: removing a character from the story must have consequences proportional to their role. Don't kill characters for shock value — kill them for story value.
