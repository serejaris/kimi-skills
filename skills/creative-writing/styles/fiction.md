# Fiction Style

Novels, novellas, short stories, flash fiction — literary and genre. The unit of quality is the **scene**; the engine is **character under pressure**.

## Voice and Tone
- Choose narrator distance deliberately (close third / first / omniscient) and hold it. Tense consistent throughout.
- Tone serves the material — wry, elegiac, tense, warm — and stays coherent across the piece.
- Literary fiction rewards precision and subtext; genre fiction rewards momentum and clear stakes. Match the user's intent.

## Structure & Format
- **Short story**: one central change. Open near the inciting moment, escalate through 2–5 scenes, turn, end on resonance — not a tidy moral. Often a single arc, one protagonist.
- **Novella / novel**: multi-arc; use the outline's plot points and per-chapter specs. Each chapter is a scene or scene-cluster with its own goal-conflict-outcome.
- **Scene shape**: a viewpoint character wants something, meets resistance, and the situation changes (for better or worse) by the scene's end. No static scenes.
- Beginnings: enter late, leave early. Endings: pay off the central promise; earn the final image.

## Language Patterns
- Show change through action and sensory specifics; name emotions sparingly.
- Distinct dialogue voices; subtext over on-the-nose statement; silence and what's left unsaid do work.
- Vary rhythm — fragments for impact, longer sentences when the scene opens out.
- One exact detail over three vague ones. Concrete nouns, active verbs.

## Prohibited Patterns
- Telling the reader how to feel ("it was a deeply moving moment").
- Adjective/adverb pile-ups; abstract emotion in place of physical beats.
- Every character sounding identical; dialogue that only conveys plot.
- Period settings with modern idioms; consistency breaks in names/timeline.
- The cumulative AI tells in content.md (synonym cycling, rule-of-three, uniform length, em-dash overuse).
